Topic: Hummingbirds

Hummingbirds are the smallest birds in the world, belonging to the family Trochilidae.
There are over 360 known species, found exclusively in the Americas, ranging from
Alaska to Tierra del Fuego.

Key facts:

- The bee hummingbird (Mellisuga helenae) of Cuba is the world's smallest bird,
  weighing roughly 1.6 grams and measuring about 5 cm in length.

- Hummingbirds can beat their wings between 10 and 80 times per second depending
  on species, producing the distinctive humming sound that gives them their name.

- Their hearts beat at up to 1,260 beats per minute during flight, dropping to
  around 50–180 beats per minute during torpor (a sleep-like state used to conserve
  energy overnight).

- Hummingbirds are the only birds capable of sustained hovering. They achieve this
  by rotating their wings in a figure-eight pattern, generating lift on both the
  forward and backward stroke.

- A hummingbird's brain makes up approximately 4.2% of its body weight — the largest
  brain-to-body ratio of any bird. This supports their exceptional spatial memory,
  allowing them to remember every flower they have visited and how long each takes
  to refill with nectar.

- They feed primarily on nectar, supplemented by small insects and spiders for
  protein. A hummingbird may visit 1,000–2,000 flowers per day.

- Ruby-throated hummingbirds (Archilochus colubris) migrate non-stop across the
  Gulf of Mexico — approximately 800 km — on a single tank of fat reserves.

- Hummingbirds cannot walk or hop; their feet are used only for perching.

- Lifespan: most species live 3–5 years in the wild; the record is over 12 years
  for a broad-tailed hummingbird banded in Colorado.
